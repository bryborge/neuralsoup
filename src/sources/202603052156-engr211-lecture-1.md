# Source: ENGR211 - Lecture 1

## tl;dr

> Introduces the fundamentals of electrical engineering, defining core circuit terminology, the historical context of the field, and the basic mathematical models and sources used in circuit theory.

## Key Topics

- **Historical Context:** Electrical Engineering emerged in the early 19th century, with Sir Francis Ronalds credited as one of the first electrical engineers for his telegraph system.
- **Circuit vs. Electronic Circuits:** "Electric" circuits focus on passive devices, while "electronic" circuits include active devices like transistors that require power.
- **Circuit Theory Assumptions:** For the mathematical models in this course, it is assumed that electrical effects are instantaneous, net charge on components is zero, and there is no magnetic coupling.
- **Circuit Architecture:** Definitions for essential components of a circuit diagram, including nodes (connection points), branches (paths between nodes), and loops/meshes.
- **Electrical Quantities:** Introduction to charge (Coulombs), current (Amperes), voltage (Volts), and power (Watts).
- **Independent vs. Dependent Sources:** Circuit sources are categorized by whether their output is fixed (independent) or controlled by another value in the circuit (dependent).

## Key Terms & Definitions

- **Passive Devices:** Components like resistors, capacitors, and inductors that do not require a power source and cannot provide gain.
- **Active Devices:** Components like transistors and integrated circuits that require power and can amplify signals.
- **Node:** An electrical connection point between two or more elements.
- **Mesh:** A closed loop that does not enclose any other loops.
- **Current (I):** The movement of electrical charges, measured in Amperes (A), where $i = dq/dt$.
- **Voltage (V):** The energy per unit of charge, measured in Volts (V), where $v = dw/dq$.
- **Ground:** A reference point used to measure and understand voltage and power values, typically designated as 0V.
- **Power (P):** The product of voltage and current ($p = vi$), measured in Watts (W).
- **Ideal Voltage Source:** A device providing a fixed voltage regardless of the current or load.
- **Ideal Current Source:** A device maintaining a set current regardless of the voltage across its terminals.
